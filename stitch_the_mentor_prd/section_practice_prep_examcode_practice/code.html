Reading Passage 1: The Future of Urban Design

timer Suggested time: 20 mins

Paragraph A. Urban design is moving away from the car-centric models of the 20th century. As cities become denser, the need for sustainable, human-scale infrastructure has never been more critical. Architects and planners are now prioritizing "walkability," a concept that ensures essential services are within a 15-minute walk of residential areas.

Paragraph B. One of the most significant challenges is retrofitting existing infrastructure. Many cities were built with wide avenues designed for high-speed traffic, which act as barriers between neighborhoods. Transforming these into green boulevards with dedicated bike lanes and widened sidewalks requires not just engineering solutions but political will.

Paragraph C. The introduction of smart technology into urban furniture is another trend. Streetlights that dim when no one is around, benches that charge phones, and waste bins that signal when they are full are becoming commonplace. However, critics argue that this "smart city" approach can lead to surveillance concerns if data privacy is not strictly regulated.

Moreover, the integration of green spaces is no longer an aesthetic choice but a public health necessity. Studies show that access to parks reduces stress and lowers urban heat island effects. Vertical gardens on skyscrapers and rooftop farms are innovative ways to introduce nature into concrete jungles where ground space is scarce.

Finally, the concept of mixed-use development is returning. Separating residential, commercial, and industrial zones, a staple of modern zoning laws, is being reconsidered. By blending these uses, cities can reduce commute times and create vibrant, 24-hour communities that are safer and more economically resilient.
